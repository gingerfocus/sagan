** Lorentz Transformation
The fundamental revelation of shyt. The alternative to the Galilean transformations: - \(x' = \gamma(x-vt)\) - \(t' = \gamma(t-\frac{vx}{c^2})\) - \(\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-\frac{v^2}{c^2}}}\)

Lorentz derived these by just guessing and checking but was later proven correct by Einstein.

Consequence #1 - Time dilation. Essentially, moving clocks will run slow. The classic example is if you have an identical twin you send on a trip at the speed of light, their trip will feel shorter for them than it actually was, so the twin on Earth is supposedly older. But, from the other one's frame of reference, the Earth is moving away from them at the speed of light, so the twin on Earth should be younger. (This is ultimately resolved by the fact that the twin's spaceship would need to turn around to come back, thereby accelerating.

Consequence #2 - Length contraction. Essentially, moving rods shrink. Observers will not agree on the lengths between things. This reconciles with the disagreement on time--- if we disagree on time but agree on speed, we must disagree on distance traveled.

Consequence #3 - Relativity of Simultaneity. Things do not necessarily happen at the same time according to observer

But, we detect a lot of muons on the ground from cosmic ray collisions, yet the upper atmosphere is much further up than 653 meters. How is this possible? It's because they're traveling so fast that the time they experience is slower than the time we, the observers, experience. Yes, that's right: time dilation. Let's go back to our equation from last class:

that distance is in fact 4633 meters, which is much more possible. Wait! But how

\(L' = L * \gamma = 653 * 7.08 = 4633\)

What is the fundamental equation proposed by *Louis de Broglie* in 1924 that suggests all matter has wave-like properties?
?
*The de Broglie Wavelength* formula:
\[\lambda = \frac{h}{p}\]
Where:
​* \(\lambda\) is the wavelength.
​* \(h\) is Planck's constant.
​* \(p\) is the momentum of the particle (\(p=mv\)).

Conceptually, how did *de Broglie's* original view of the "matter wave" differ from *Schrödinger's* later interpretation?
?
The particle does not have a defined position until measurement; the wave describes the probability of finding it.
​* *de Broglie (Pilot Wave):* Originally envisioned a physical wave that guides a distinct particle (a dualistic realism). (One particle, many position)
​* *Schrödinger (Copenhagen view):* The mathematical wave function \(\Psi\) is *not* a physical disturbance in space, but a /probability amplitude/. (One unknown position)

Which principle dictates the fundamental limit of precision when measuring wave-particle properties?
?
*Heisenberg's Uncertainty Principle*. It states that the more precisely you know the position (\(x\)), the less precisely you can know the momentum (\(p\)), and vice versa.
\[\sigma_x \sigma_p \ge \frac{\hbar}{2}\]
This is a direct consequence of the wave nature of matter (Fourier transform properties).

In the *Copenhagen Interpretation*, what happens to a system existing in a /superposition/ of states when an observer measures it?
?
*Wave Function Collapse*. The continuous evolution of the wave function (governed by the Schrödinger equation) is interrupted. The system instantaneously "collapses" or reduces to a single *eigenstate* corresponding to the measured value.

How does the *Many-Worlds Interpretation (Everettian)* handle the concept of "State Collapse" differently than Copenhagen?
?
*MWI denies that collapse occurs.*
Instead of the wave function collapsing to a single outcome, the universe *branches*. All possible outcomes of a quantum interaction are realized in separate, non-communicating branches of the universe. The observer becomes entangled with the system, existing in a superposition of observing all outcomes.

What is the time-dependent equation that describes how the quantum state of a physical system changes over time?
?
*The Schrödinger Equation:*
\[i\hbar \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\Psi(x,t) = \hat{H}\Psi(x,t)\]
Where \(\hat{H}\) is the Hamiltonian operator (total energy). This equation treats the particle entirely as a wave function (\(\Psi\)) evolving deterministically---until measurement.

If \(\Psi\) represents the wave function of a particle, how do we calculate the probability of finding that particle in a specific region?
?
By using the *Born Rule*. The probability density is the square of the absolute magnitude of the wave function:
\[P(x) = |\Psi(x)|^2 = \Psi^*(x)\Psi(x)\]
This bridged the gap between the wave mathematics and the particle observation.

In the double-slit experiment, what happens if you fire electrons one by one, but place a *detector* at the slits to see which one the electron goes through?
?
The *Interference Pattern disappears*.
+ *Unobserved:* Electrons act as [[file:waves.org][waves]], interfering with themselves to create a striped pattern.
+ *Observed:* The act of measurement collapses the wave function; electrons act as particles and form two distinct clumps (clumps corresponding to Slit A and Slit B).

What is *Niels Bohr's* principle regarding the observation of wave and particle properties?
?
The Principle of Complementarity.
It states that objects have complementary properties (like wave behavior and particle behavior) which cannot be observed or measured *simultaneously*.
The experimental setup determines which property is manifested; you can see the wave /or/ the particle, but never both at the exact same time.
